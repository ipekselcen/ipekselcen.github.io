Background

DNA methyltransferase 1 (Dnmt1) maintains DNA methylation patterns during cell division, playing a critical role in silencing repetitive elements and regulating gene expression. Loss of Dnmt1 leads to global hypomethylation and can trigger developmental defects and genomic instability.

I analyzed publicly available RNA-seq data (GSE79018) comparing mouse embryonic stem cells with and without Dnmt1 to understand the transcriptional consequences of losing this key epigenetic regulator.

🔬 Key Findings

  • Widespread transcriptional changes: 347 genes showed significant differential expression (FDR < 0.05, |log₂FC| > 1)
  • Reactivation of pluripotency factors: Upregulation of Pou5f1 (Oct4), Sox2, and Nanog—core pluripotency transcription factors
  • Chromatin remodeling response: Enrichment of genes involved in histone modification and chromatin organization
  • Compensation mechanisms: Upregulation of other DNA methyltransferases (Dnmt3a, Dnmt3b) suggesting compensatory responses
  • Metabolic reprogramming: Changes in one-carbon metabolism and methionine cycle genes

Biological Interpretation

Reversion to Pluripotent State

The upregulation of core pluripotency factors (Oct4, Sox2, Nanog) suggests that loss of Dnmt1-mediated DNA methylation allows reactivation of genes normally silenced during differentiation. This is consistent with the known role of DNA methylation in maintaining cellular identity and preventing dedifferentiation.

Compensatory Epigenetic Mechanisms

Upregulation of Dnmt3a and Dnmt3b indicates an attempt to compensate for loss of maintenance methylation. However, de novo methyltransferases cannot fully replace Dnmt1's role in maintaining established methylation patterns, leading to progressive hypomethylation.

Chromatin Remodeling Response

The enrichment of chromatin organization genes suggests active remodeling of the epigenome in response to hypomethylation. This likely reflects both direct consequences of methylation loss and cellular attempts to restore proper gene regulation through alternative mechanisms.

Analysis Approach

I processed raw sequencing reads through a standard RNA-seq pipeline: quality control with FastQC/MultiQC, adapter trimming with Trim Galore, alignment to mouse reference genome (GRCm39) using STAR, and read quantification with featureCounts.

Differential expression analysis was performed using DESeq2 with standard normalization and FDR correction. Pathway enrichment used clusterProfiler for GO and KEGG analyses. All visualizations were created in R using ggplot2, ComplexHeatmap, and EnhancedVolcano.

Connection to My Research

This analysis demonstrates the same RNA-seq workflow I used in my PhD research on TET2-mediated RNA modifications. Like Dnmt1, TET2 is an epigenetic regulator, and understanding how loss of these enzymes affects transcription is central to my work.

The methods here—from quality control through pathway analysis—have been validated in my published research in Cell Reports and Journal of Cell Biology, where similar approaches revealed how TET2 regulates alternative splicing in oligodendrocyte progenitor cells.

📊 Technical Details & QC Metrics
  • Samples: 4 (2 Control, 2 Dnmt1 KO)
  • Sequencing: Illumina paired-end, ~30M reads per sample
  • Reference: Mouse GRCm39 (Ensembl release 115)
  • Alignment rate: 85-90% uniquely mapped
  • Gene assignment: 72% of reads assigned to genes
  • DE criteria: FDR < 0.05, |log₂FC| > 1
  • Tools: STAR v2.7.11a, featureCounts v2.0.6, DESeq2 v1.42.0
