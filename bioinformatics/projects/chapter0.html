RNA-seq Analysis: Understanding Your Options and Making Informed Decisions

A conceptual guide to help biologists navigate the RNA-seq analysis landscape. Learn the "why" behind different approaches before diving into the "how."

Who is this for?

This guide is designed for experimental biologists who need to analyze RNA-seq data but feel overwhelmed by the many different tools and approaches. We focus on understanding the decision points rather than memorizing commands.

No prior bioinformatics experience required. We'll explain concepts using real-world analogies and help you make informed choices for your specific research questions.

1. Introduction: One Question, Many Paths

At its core, RNA-seq answers a deceptively simple question: How much is each gene expressed in my samples?

But when you sit down to analyze your data, you're immediately confronted with choices:

  • Should I use STAR, HISAT2, Salmon, or Kallisto?
  • Do I want gene-level or transcript-level quantification?
  • What's the difference between FPKM, TPM, and normalized counts?
  • How do I know if I'm making the right choice?

Here's the good news: there's no single "correct" pipeline. Different approaches work better for different biological questions, sample types, and computational resources.

The Goal of This Guide

Rather than telling you "always use method X," we'll help you understand:

  • What each approach actually does (in plain English)
  • When to use which method
  • What trade-offs you're making
  • How to recognize if something went wrong

Think of this guide as your RNA-seq GPS. We'll show you the different routes, explain what makes each one unique, and help you choose the path that makes sense for your specific destination.

⚠️ Setting Expectations: What RNA-seq Can and Cannot Tell You

RNA-seq CAN tell you:

  • Which genes are transcribed in your samples
  • How transcript levels change between conditions
  • Which biological pathways might be affected
  • What questions to ask next in the lab

RNA-seq CANNOT tell you:

  • Protein levels or protein activity
  • Whether changes are cause, consequence, or compensation
  • Biological mechanism or molecular function
  • What to do therapeutically

This tutorial focuses on the computational pipeline—getting from raw data to a statistically sound gene list. But the most important skill—biological interpretation and experimental validation—comes from understanding your biological system deeply. You are a biologist who uses computational tools, not the other way around.

2. Decision Point 1: Alignment-Based vs. Alignment-Free Quantification

This is your first major fork in the road. Do you align your reads to the genome, or do you skip that step entirely?

The Two Approaches

Alignment-based (STAR, HISAT2): Map each read to its exact location in the genome, then count how many reads land in each gene.

Alignment-free (Salmon, Kallisto): Match reads directly to known transcript sequences without pinpointing genomic locations. This is sometimes called "pseudo-alignment" or "lightweight alignment."

The Library Analogy

Imagine you're trying to figure out which books are most popular in a library by analyzing borrowing records (where each record is like a sequencing read).

Alignment-based approach: You first figure out exactly where each book sits on the shelves (chromosome coordinates), then count how many borrowing records point to books in each section. This gives you a complete map of the library, but it takes more time.

Alignment-free approach: You match borrowing records directly to a catalog of book titles, skipping the shelf location step entirely. Much faster, but you lose the spatial information.

When to Use What

Use Salmon or Kallisto when:

  • Your main question is: "Which genes are differentially expressed?"
  • You're working with a well-annotated organism (human, mouse, etc.)
  • You have many samples (>50) and speed matters
  • Computational resources are limited
  • You want transcript-level quantification in addition to gene-level

Use STAR or HISAT2 when:

  • You need to discover novel transcripts or splicing events
  • You want to visualize your reads in IGV (Integrative Genomics Viewer)
  • You're working with a poorly annotated organism
  • You need the alignment for other analyses (variant calling, RNA editing)
  • You plan to integrate RNA-seq with other genomic data (ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq)
  • You're investigating specific genomic regions or mutations

A Note on Accuracy

Multiple benchmarking studies have shown that Salmon and Kallisto produce gene-level counts that are highly concordant with alignment-based methods for differential expression analysis. The key difference is really about what else you need beyond just the counts.

Understanding the Tools

STAR (Spliced Transcripts Alignment to a Reference)

  • Strength: Excellent at detecting splice junctions, including novel ones
  • Two-pass mode: First pass discovers junctions, second pass uses them for better alignment
  • Trade-off: High memory requirements but very accurate
  • Output: BAM files you can open in IGV to see exactly where reads mapped

HISAT2 (Hierarchical Indexing for Spliced Alignment)

  • Strength: Much lower memory footprint than STAR
  • Speed: Faster than STAR for most analyses
  • Trade-off: Slightly less sensitive for novel junction discovery
  • Best use: When you need BAM files but have limited computational resources

Salmon

  • Strength: Extremely fast while maintaining accuracy
  • Built-in features: Automatic bias correction, handles multi-mapping probabilistically
  • Output: Transcript-level counts (can be summarized to genes)
  • Trade-off: Requires a reference transcriptome (can't discover novel transcripts)

Kallisto

  • Strength: Even faster than Salmon
  • Unique feature: Bootstrapping for uncertainty quantification
  • Method: Uses k-mers for pseudoalignment
  • Best use: Large studies where speed is critical

3. Decision Point 2: Gene-Level vs. Transcript-Level Analysis

This decision shapes what biological questions you can answer with your data.

Understanding the Difference

Gene-level analysis asks: "How much is the entire DNMT1 gene expressed?"
Transcript-level analysis asks: "Which specific versions (isoforms) of DNMT1 are expressed?"

The Restaurant Menu Analogy

Imagine you manage a restaurant and want to understand what customers order:

Gene-level thinking: "How many pasta dishes did we sell today?" (Total pasta orders = ~100)

Transcript-level thinking: "Which specific pasta dishes did customers order?" (Spaghetti = 45, Penne = 30, Rigatoni = 15, Fettuccine = 10)

Both answers are correct, but they tell you different things. The gene-level view is simpler and tells you about overall popularity. The transcript-level view reveals customer preferences for specific variations.

When Does This Matter?

Gene-level quantification is sufficient when:

  • Your question is about overall gene regulation (up or down)
  • You're interested in which pathways are affected
  • You're doing a first-pass exploratory analysis
  • Alternative splicing isn't relevant to your biological system
  • You want simpler statistics and clearer interpretation

Transcript-level analysis becomes important when:

  • Alternative splicing is relevant to your biology (development, cancer, neurodegeneration)
  • Different isoforms have different functions (e.g., membrane-bound vs. secreted)
  • You're studying tissue-specific isoform expression
  • Protein domains are included/excluded by splicing
  • Known splicing defects are involved in your disease model

Real-World Example

Consider the gene BCL2L1, which encodes two major isoforms:

  • BCL-xL: Anti-apoptotic (prevents cell death)
  • BCL-xS: Pro-apoptotic (promotes cell death)

Gene-level analysis might show "no change in BCL2L1 expression," but transcript-level analysis could reveal a dramatic shift from the long (protective) to the short (death-promoting) isoform. This would completely change your biological interpretation!

The Statistical Trade-offs

Challenges with Transcript-Level Analysis

Transcript-level analysis is statistically harder:

  • More features = more multiple testing: Testing 20,000 genes vs. 200,000 transcripts means more stringent p-value corrections
  • Lower counts per feature: If a gene has 1000 reads split across 5 transcripts, each transcript has ~200 reads (less statistical power)
  • Correlation structure: Transcripts from the same gene aren't independent, violating some statistical assumptions
  • Annotation ambiguity: Some reads can't be uniquely assigned to one transcript

The Typical Workflows

Gene-level workflow:

# Alignment-based: STAR/HISAT2 → featureCounts/HTSeq → DESeq2 → Gene-level DE # Alignment-free: Salmon/Kallisto → tximport (summarize to genes) → DESeq2 → Gene-level DE

Transcript-level workflow:

# Full isoform analysis: Salmon/Kallisto → IsoformSwitchAnalyzeR → Isoform switching # Differential transcript usage: Salmon/Kallisto → DRIMSeq/DEXSeq → Which isoforms change within genes # Discovery-based: STAR → StringTie → Ballgown → Transcript-level DE

Practical Advice

Start with gene-level analysis. It's simpler, more powerful, and answers most biological questions. If you find interesting candidates or if alternative splicing is central to your question, then dive into transcript-level analysis for those specific genes or pathways.

4. Decision Point 3: Count Quantification Methods

If you chose an alignment-based approach, you now need to convert your aligned reads (BAM files) into a count matrix. This step determines how many reads "belong" to each gene.

The Main Tools

featureCounts (from Subread package)

Most popular choice for a reason:

  • Very fast (multi-threaded)
  • Handles paired-end reads elegantly
  • Clear handling of multi-mapping reads
  • Produces a simple count matrix ready for DESeq2
  • Excellent documentation

HTSeq

The original standard:

  • Python-based, well-established
  • Multiple counting modes (union, intersection-strict, intersection-nonempty)
  • Slower than featureCounts (single-threaded)
  • Still widely used and cited

RSEM

When you need transcript-level from aligned reads:

  • Uses Expectation-Maximization algorithm
  • Probabilistically assigns multi-mapping reads
  • Provides both gene and isoform-level counts
  • More computationally intensive

The Multi-Mapping Reads Question

Some reads map equally well to multiple locations in the genome (common for gene families, repetitive regions, or similar isoforms). How do you count these?

Strategy What it does When to use
Discard them Only count uniquely mapping reads Conservative approach for gene-level DE (most common)
Count fractionally If read maps to 3 places, give 0.33 count to each When working with gene families or pseudogenes
Count all Give full count to every location Risky - inflates counts, rarely recommended

Practical Recommendation

For standard differential expression analysis, use featureCounts with default settings (discarding multi-mappers). This is conservative and works well for most biological questions. The small number of reads you lose is unlikely to affect your biological conclusions.

5. Decision Point 4: Normalization - Making Data Comparable

Raw sequencing produces different numbers of reads for each sample (library size differences). Additionally, longer genes capture more reads than shorter genes, even if they're expressed at the same level. Normalization corrects for these technical biases.

Understanding the Metrics

Raw Counts

What they are: The direct output from alignment and quantification
When to use: As input to DESeq2 or edgeR for statistical testing
Never use for: Comparing across samples or genes

CPM (Counts Per Million)

What it does: Divides counts by total library size and multiplies by 1 million
Formula: (count / total_reads) × 1,000,000
Use for: Quick visual comparisons
Limitation: Doesn't account for gene length or compositional bias

FPKM/RPKM (Fragments/Reads Per Kilobase per Million)

What it does: Adjusts for both library size AND gene length
Formula: (count / gene_length_kb) / (total_reads / 1,000,000)
Historical note: Was once popular, now largely deprecated
Problem: Biased for differential expression (do NOT use for DE analysis)

TPM (Transcripts Per Million)

What it does: Like FPKM but does normalization in a different order
Key advantage: TPM values sum to 1 million in each sample (interpretable)
Use for: Comparing relative abundances across samples
Still not ideal for: Statistical DE testing (use raw counts with DESeq2 instead)

TMM/RLE (Used internally by edgeR/DESeq2)

What they do: Sophisticated normalization that assumes most genes don't change
TMM: Trimmed Mean of M-values (edgeR)
RLE: Relative Log Expression (DESeq2)
Key point: These tools apply normalization automatically - you provide raw counts

Critical: What to Use When

For differential expression testing: Always provide RAW COUNTS to DESeq2 or edgeR. They handle normalization internally.

For visualization: Use DESeq2's normalized counts (via counts(dds, normalized=TRUE))

For reporting expression levels: TPM is more interpretable than FPKM

Never: Use FPKM or TPM as input to DESeq2 or for statistical testing

Why This Matters: A Concrete Example

Imagine you have two samples:

  • Sample A: 20 million reads total
  • Sample B: 40 million reads total (deeper sequencing)

Gene X has:

  • Sample A: 100 raw counts
  • Sample B: 200 raw counts

Is Gene X differentially expressed? No! It just got twice as many reads because Sample B was sequenced twice as deeply.

After CPM normalization:

  • Sample A: (100 / 20M) × 1M = 5 CPM
  • Sample B: (200 / 40M) × 1M = 5 CPM

Now they're comparable - Gene X has the same expression level in both samples.

6. Decision Point 5: Experimental Design Considerations

The best analysis pipeline won't save a poorly designed experiment. Here are key considerations that affect your analysis strategy:

Batch Effects

What they are: Technical variation from processing samples at different times, on different days, or with different reagent lots

When to worry about batch effects:

  • Samples processed on different days
  • Different library prep kits or batches
  • Samples sequenced on different runs
  • Different people prepared the libraries

How to detect: Run PCA and check if samples cluster by processing date rather than biological condition

How to handle:

  • Best solution: Balanced experimental design (equal representation of conditions in each batch)
  • If design is balanced: Include batch as a covariate in DESeq2 design formula
  • If design is unbalanced: Use ComBat-seq or RUVSeq for batch correction
  • Last resort: limma's removeBatchEffect (only for visualization, not for DE testing)

Replicates: How Many Do You Need?

Replicates per group Power to detect Recommendation
n = 2 Only very large effects Avoid if possible - minimal statistical power
n = 3 Moderate to large effects Minimum acceptable for most studies
n = 4-5 Good power for typical effects Recommended for most experiments
n = 6+ Excellent power, can detect subtle effects Ideal, especially for heterogeneous samples

Rule of Thumb

It's better to have more biological replicates than more sequencing depth. 6 samples with 20M reads each will give you more statistical power than 3 samples with 40M reads each.

Paired vs. Unpaired Designs

Paired design: Same subject/sample measured before and after treatment (e.g., tumor vs. adjacent normal tissue from the same patient)

Advantage: Controls for individual variation - much more powerful

How to handle in DESeq2: Include subject ID in the design formula:

# For paired design: design = ~ patient + condition # This controls for patient-to-patient variation

7. Recommended Pipelines for Common Scenarios

Now that you understand the options, here are concrete recommendations for different research scenarios:

Scenario 1: Standard Differential Expression (Like Dnmt1 Project)

Question: Which genes change between condition A and condition B?

Recommended Pipeline:

Salmon/Kallisto → tximport → DESeq2 → Gene-level results

Why this works: Fast, accurate, and perfectly adequate for identifying differentially expressed genes. The speed advantage of Salmon/Kallisto lets you iterate quickly if you need to re-analyze.

Scenario 2: Novel Transcript Discovery

Question: Are there unannotated transcripts in my system?

Recommended Pipeline:

STAR (2-pass mode) → StringTie (assembly) → Merge annotations → featureCounts/StringTie quantification → DESeq2

Why this works: STAR's 2-pass mode discovers novel splice junctions. StringTie assembles transcripts including unannotated ones. You get both discovery and quantification.

Scenario 3: Alternative Splicing / Isoform Analysis

Question: Do different isoforms of my gene of interest change?

Recommended Pipeline:

Salmon → IsoformSwitchAnalyzeR → Isoform switching analysis Alternative: STAR → rMATS → Differential splicing events

Why this works: IsoformSwitchAnalyzeR specifically identifies isoform switches and their functional consequences. rMATS is excellent for detecting specific splicing event types (exon skipping, intron retention, etc.).

Scenario 4: Large Cohort Study (>100 samples)

Question: Gene expression patterns across many patient samples

Recommended Pipeline:

Kallisto → tximport → DESeq2 (or limma-voom for even larger datasets)

Why this works: Kallisto is the fastest option. When you have many samples, you have plenty of statistical power, so the marginal gains from more sophisticated alignment aren't worth the time cost.

Scenario 5: Integration with ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq

Question: How do chromatin changes relate to expression changes?

Recommended Pipeline:

STAR → featureCounts → DESeq2 (Keep BAM files for visualization in IGV alongside ChIP-seq/ATAC-seq)

Why this works: You need BAM files to visualize RNA-seq coverage at specific loci (promoters, enhancers) alongside your ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq peaks. Salmon/Kallisto don't provide this.

Scenario 6: Non-Model Organism or Poor Annotation

Question: Analyzing RNA-seq from an organism with incomplete genome annotation

Recommended Pipeline:

HISAT2 (or STAR if memory allows) → StringTie (genome-guided assembly) → featureCounts with custom GTF → DESeq2

Why this works: You can't rely on existing annotations, so you build a transcriptome from your data. HISAT2 gives you alignment flexibility with reasonable memory usage.

8. Our Choices for the Dnmt1 Tutorial

For the detailed Dnmt1 knockout RNA-seq tutorial that follows this guide, we made these specific choices:

Pipeline: STAR → featureCounts → DESeq2

Why STAR (not Salmon/Kallisto)?

  • Educational value: Understanding alignment teaches fundamental concepts about how sequencing reads map to genomes
  • Flexibility: BAM files enable visualization in IGV, which is crucial for validating results
  • Future integration: BAM files allow integration with ChIP-seq or ATAC-seq data from the same cells
  • Discovery potential: 2-pass mode can identify novel splicing events relevant to OPC biology

Why Gene-Level (not Transcript-Level)?

  • Primary question: "What genes does Dnmt1 regulate?" rather than "What isoforms change?"
  • Statistical power: Gene-level analysis has more power to detect moderate effect sizes
  • Interpretability: Gene-level results are easier to validate experimentally and connect to pathways
  • Practical approach: Start broad, then investigate specific genes for isoform differences if interesting candidates emerge

Why featureCounts?

  • Speed: Handles multiple BAM files quickly with multi-threading
  • Paired-end handling: Correctly counts fragments (not individual reads) for paired-end data
  • Clear output: Simple count matrix format that DESeq2 expects
  • Standard choice: Most widely used tool for this step

Why DESeq2?

  • Gold standard: Most widely used and trusted tool for RNA-seq differential expression
  • Robust statistics: Handles small sample sizes well, robust to outliers
  • Excellent documentation: Detailed vignettes and active support community
  • Integrated workflow: From normalization through visualization in one framework

What This Approach Gives Us

This pipeline provides:

  • Reliable differential expression results for gene-level analysis
  • BAM files for visual validation of interesting findings
  • Foundation for potential follow-up analyses (isoforms, integration with other data types)
  • Standard, reproducible workflow that reviewers and readers will recognize

9. Common Pitfalls to Avoid

❌ Using FPKM or TPM for Differential Expression

Why it's wrong: These normalized values violate the statistical assumptions of DE tools

Do this instead: Always provide raw counts to DESeq2 or edgeR

❌ Filtering Genes After Normalization

Why it's wrong: Normalization factors are calculated based on all genes, including low-count ones

Do this instead: Filter low-count genes first, then run DESeq2

❌ Comparing Gene Expression Across Genes Without Proper Normalization

Why it's wrong: Raw counts favor longer genes (they capture more reads)

Do this instead: Use TPM or FPKM only when comparing expression levels of different genes within a sample

❌ Ignoring Batch Effects

Why it's wrong: Technical variation can completely obscure biological signal

Do this instead: Always run PCA first, check if samples cluster by processing date, include batch in model if needed

❌ Not Checking Alignment Rates

Why it's wrong: Low alignment rates indicate problems (wrong genome, contamination, poor quality)

Do this instead: Check that >80% of reads align before proceeding with DE analysis

❌ Using Transcript Counts Directly in DESeq2

Why it's wrong: DESeq2 expects gene-level counts or proper transcript-level tools

Do this instead: Use tximport to summarize transcript counts to gene-level, or use specialized tools for transcript-level DE

❌ Over-Interpreting Small Fold Changes

Why it's wrong: Statistical significance (low p-value) doesn't mean biological importance

Do this instead: Set reasonable fold-change thresholds (e.g., log2FC > 1) based on your biological system

❌ Treating Replicates as Independent Samples

Why it's wrong: Technical replicates (same sample sequenced twice) aren't independent biological measurements

Do this instead: Merge technical replicates; only biological replicates provide statistical power

❌ Stopping at the Gene List

Why it's wrong: RNA-seq is a discovery tool, not an endpoint. mRNA ≠ protein ≠ function. Getting a statistically significant gene list is the beginning of your research, not the end.

Do this instead: Validate top hits with RT-qPCR, confirm protein levels with Western blots, test function with perturbation experiments

❌ Assuming RNA Changes Predict Protein Changes

Why it's wrong: Post-transcriptional regulation, translation efficiency, protein stability, and localization are all invisible to RNA-seq. Many highly upregulated mRNAs produce little to no protein.

Do this instead: Measure protein levels directly for genes of interest (Western blot, immunofluorescence, mass spectrometry)

❌ Over-relying on Pathway Enrichment

Why it's wrong: GO terms are biased toward well-studied processes, pathways overlap extensively, and statistical enrichment doesn't equal biological importance

Do this instead: Use pathway analysis to generate hypotheses about biological processes, then validate specific genes from those pathways experimentally

❌ Confusing Correlation with Causation

Why it's wrong: Just because Gene X is upregulated in your condition doesn't mean it's driving the phenotype—it could be a consequence, a compensation attempt, or completely unrelated

Do this instead: Design perturbation experiments (knockdown/knockout or overexpression) to test whether candidate genes are functionally important

10. Beyond the Gene List: Biological Interpretation

You've run your pipeline perfectly. DESeq2 gave you a list of 500 differentially expressed genes with padj < 0.05 and beautiful volcano plots. Congratulations! Now comes the hard part: What does it actually mean biologically?

The Most Critical Lesson

RNA-seq tells you what's transcribed, NOT what's functional.

This entire tutorial has helped you generate a high-quality gene list with statistical rigor. But that gene list is just the beginning. The hardest work—and the most important work—happens next: figuring out what those changes mean for your biological system.

The Central Dogma Doesn't Always Hold

DNA → RNA → Protein → Function

This is what we teach in introductory biology. Reality is much messier:

  • MicroRNAs and RNA-binding proteins can completely block translation even when mRNA is abundant
  • Protein half-life varies enormously: Histones are stable for days, transcription factors for minutes. A 2-fold increase in mRNA might mean nothing if the protein is already saturated or if it's degraded immediately.
  • Post-translational modifications (phosphorylation, acetylation, ubiquitination) completely change protein function without touching RNA levels at all
  • Subcellular localization matters: A protein in the wrong compartment is non-functional, but RNA-seq can't tell you where proteins go
  • Protein-protein interactions: Many proteins need binding partners to be active—RNA-seq won't show you this

Questions to Ask About Your Results

1. Is this upregulation cause, consequence, or compensation?

Example from oligodendrocyte biology: Imagine you knock out Dnmt1 and see upregulation of Dnmt3a and Dnmt3b.

  • Cause? No—you knocked out Dnmt1 first, so the other DNMTs didn't cause the initial effect
  • Compensation? Possibly—cells might be attempting to restore DNA methylation function through alternative methyltransferases
  • Consequence? Maybe—loss of DNA methylation might trigger a chromatin stress response that includes upregulating related enzymes
  • Bystander effect? Perhaps—these genes might be in an open chromatin region that becomes derepressed for unrelated reasons

How to distinguish: You need functional experiments. Try knocking down the "compensatory" genes and measuring DNA methylation levels. Check protein levels and enzymatic activity. Measure the timeline—do these genes go up before or after the phenotype appears?

2. Does the direction of change make biological sense?

Biological Sanity Checks

Before getting excited about statistical significance:

  • If you knocked out a transcriptional repressor, are its known target genes upregulated?
  • If you're studying differentiation, do stem cell markers go down and differentiation markers go up?
  • Are housekeeping genes stable? (If Gapdh, Actb, or ribosomal proteins are wildly changing, something's wrong)
  • Do positive controls behave as expected? (If you know Gene X should change based on previous work, does it?)
  • Check known biology: Do your top hits have any connection to your phenotype in the literature?

3. What's the effect size, and does it matter biologically?

Statistical significance and biological significance are not the same thing.

Scenario A: Gene with padj = 1e-50 and log2FC = 0.3

  • Statistically: Extremely significant (amazing p-value!)
  • Biologically: Probably meaningless—1.23-fold change is unlikely to drive phenotypes in most systems

Scenario B: Gene with padj = 0.04 and log2FC = 4 (16-fold change)

  • Statistically: Barely significant (just passes your threshold)
  • Biologically: Very interesting—massive changes like this deserve investigation even with borderline statistics

Context matters: What fold-changes are typical in your system?

  • Development/differentiation: 2-3 fold changes can completely alter cell fate
  • Metabolic stress: You might see 50-fold changes in stress response genes that are ultimately inconsequential
  • Your specific system: Only you know what's normal—use that biological knowledge!

The Problem with Pathway Enrichment

GO Terms and KEGG Pathways Are Useful Tools, But Dangerous Shortcuts

The temptation: "My top enriched pathway is 'chromatin organization' with p < 0.001, so I'll write in my paper that Dnmt1 regulates chromatin organization!"

Why this is problematic:

  • Annotation bias: Well-studied processes (chromatin, cell cycle, apoptosis) have hundreds of genes annotated. Understudied processes have few. This creates artifactual enrichment.
  • Circular logic: You're studying an epigenetic regulator, so of course epigenetic genes are in your list—that doesn't necessarily mean it's the mechanism
  • Pathway crosstalk: A gene annotated in "chromatin remodeling" is also in "DNA repair," "cell cycle regulation," "transcription," and five other categories
  • Statistical issues: Standard enrichment tests don't account for gene length, expression level, or correlation structure
  • Vague categories: What does "regulation of biological process" or "cellular response to stimulus" actually tell you?

Use pathway analysis as a hypothesis generator, not as your conclusion.

Designing Follow-Up Experiments

Your RNA-seq is a discovery tool that generates hypotheses. Those hypotheses need testing. Here's a roadmap:

Validation tier 1: Confirm the RNA-seq is correct

  1. RT-qPCR on the same RNA samples: Pick 5-10 top hits, validate that the fold-changes match. This confirms your RNA-seq pipeline worked correctly.
  2. RT-qPCR on independent biological replicates: Process new samples from scratch. This confirms the effect is reproducible, not a batch effect.

If these don't validate, stop and troubleshoot your RNA-seq before proceeding.

Validation tier 2: Check if RNA changes translate to protein

  1. Western blot: For your top candidates, do protein levels match RNA levels? (They often don't!)
  2. Immunofluorescence: Protein level AND subcellular localization—where is the protein?
  3. Mass spectrometry: If you have resources, unbiased proteomics tells you what's actually changing at the protein level

Key insight: If RNA goes up 5-fold but protein doesn't change, the RNA change probably doesn't matter functionally.

Validation tier 3: Test functional importance

  1. Knockdown/knockout of candidates: Does perturbing Gene X recapitulate any aspect of your original phenotype?
  2. Overexpression: Does forcing Gene X expression rescue your knockout phenotype?
  3. Functional assays: Measure the actual biological output (proliferation, differentiation, migration, etc.)

Mechanistic experiments: Understanding "how"

  • ChIP-seq or CUT&RUN: Is your factor of interest directly binding to the promoters of changed genes? Or is this an indirect effect?
  • ATAC-seq: Are expression changes accompanied by chromatin accessibility changes?
  • Time-course experiments: What changes first? This helps distinguish cause from consequence.
  • Rescue experiments: If Gene A knockout causes Gene B upregulation, does re-expressing Gene A bring it back down?
  • Epistasis analysis: Double knockouts can reveal genetic interactions and pathway order

Integration with Other Data Types

RNA-seq alone can't tell you mechanism—it only tells you association.

The most powerful insights come from integrating multiple data types:

Combine RNA-seq with... To answer... Example question
ChIP-seq Which changes are direct targets vs. downstream effects? "Does Dnmt1 directly bind genes that change in the knockout?"
ATAC-seq Are expression changes driven by chromatin accessibility? "Do upregulated genes have newly accessible promoters?"
Proteomics Do RNA changes translate to protein changes? "Which RNA changes actually matter for protein levels?"
CRISPR screens Which genes are functionally important for the phenotype? "Of 200 upregulated genes, which ones drive differentiation?"
scRNA-seq Are changes uniform or cell-type-specific? "Is this happening in all OPCs or a subpopulation?"
Metabolomics Do expression changes affect cellular metabolism? "Does upregulation of metabolic genes change actual metabolite levels?"

Example: Interpreting a Dnmt1 Knockout Experiment

RNA-seq finding: 347 differentially expressed genes, with enrichment for "chromatin organization" and upregulation of Dnmt3a/3b

What RNA-seq tells you:

  • Transcript levels changed for these genes
  • The changes are statistically significant
  • Chromatin-related genes are over-represented

What RNA-seq does NOT tell you:

  • Are these direct Dnmt1 targets or indirect downstream effects?
  • Do protein levels of Dnmt3a/3b actually increase?
  • Is the Dnmt3a/3b upregulation compensatory (trying to restore function) or pathological (stress response)?
  • Would preventing Dnmt3a/3b upregulation rescue the phenotype?
  • What's the mechanism connecting Dnmt1 loss to these expression changes?

How to find out (follow-up experiments):

  • ChIP-seq for Dnmt1: Does it directly bind promoters of upregulated genes? (Answers: direct vs. indirect)
  • Western blots for Dnmt3a/3b: Do protein levels match the RNA increase? (Answers: translation efficiency)
  • Dnmt3a/3b knockdown in Dnmt1 KO cells: Does blocking the upregulation worsen or improve the phenotype? (Answers: compensatory vs. pathological)
  • Time-course RNA-seq: What changes first after Dnmt1 loss? (Answers: cause vs. consequence)
  • DNA methylation profiling: Which genomic regions lose methylation? Do those correlate with gene expression changes? (Answers: mechanism)

Common Biological Interpretation Mistakes

❌ "This gene is upregulated, so it must be important for the phenotype"

Why it's wrong: Correlation ≠ causation. The gene might be:

  • A bystander (changed for unrelated reasons)
  • A consequence (response to the phenotype, not cause of it)
  • A failed compensation attempt (cell trying to fix the problem but failing)
  • A stress response (non-specific reaction to perturbation)

Do this instead: Test function with loss-of-function or gain-of-function experiments

❌ "The RNA went up 5-fold, so the protein probably went up 5-fold too"

Why it's wrong: Post-transcriptional regulation is pervasive:

  • MicroRNAs can block translation
  • mRNA might be sequestered in stress granules
  • Protein might be rapidly degraded
  • Translation might be selectively inhibited

Studies comparing RNA-seq and proteomics typically find only 30-50% correlation between mRNA and protein levels.

Do this instead: Measure protein directly for genes you care about (Western, IF, mass spec)

❌ "This pathway is enriched, so that's THE mechanism"

Why it's wrong:

  • Pathway enrichment is a statistical observation about your gene list, not a statement about biology
  • Most genes belong to multiple pathways
  • The "pathway" might be too broad to be meaningful
  • Enrichment could be an artifact of annotation bias

Do this instead: Use pathway analysis to identify specific candidate genes, then validate those genes individually

❌ "500 genes changed, so I need to study all 500"

Why it's wrong: Most gene expression changes are downstream ripple effects. The key drivers are usually a small subset.

Do this instead: Prioritize genes based on:

  • Magnitude of change: Larger fold-changes are more likely to be functionally important
  • Known biology: Genes with established roles in your system
  • Direct targets: If you have ChIP-seq, focus on direct binding targets
  • Druggability: For translational work, genes that can be therapeutically targeted
  • Validation feasibility: Genes where you have good antibodies or reagents

The Iterative Nature of Discovery

Good biology is iterative. RNA-seq is rarely a one-and-done experiment:

The Scientific Method in Practice

  1. Initial hypothesis: "Dnmt1 is important for oligodendrocyte differentiation"
  2. RNA-seq experiment: Knock out Dnmt1, discover what changes
  3. Analysis: Generate statistically sound gene list (what you learned in this tutorial)
  4. Validation: Confirm key findings with RT-qPCR and Western blots
  5. Refined hypothesis: "Dnmt1 regulates OPC differentiation through direct repression of pluripotency genes and chromatin remodelers"
  6. Mechanistic experiments: ChIP-seq shows Dnmt1 binds these promoters; knockdown confirms they're functionally important
  7. Model building: Dnmt1 maintains DNA methylation at specific loci to repress alternative cell fates
  8. Test predictions: Does your model predict other phenotypes? Can you rescue with downstream targets?
  9. Additional RNA-seq: Time-course or perturbation of downstream targets to refine the model

RNA-seq appears at steps 2, 9, and potentially more times as you iterate.

Your Expertise Makes the Difference

Here's what makes a mediocre RNA-seq paper versus an excellent one:

Mediocre approach:

  • Run RNA-seq → Get gene list → Run GO enrichment → Write: "We found enrichment for chromatin organization, suggesting Dnmt1 regulates chromatin"
  • Stop there, never validate anything
  • Circular conclusion (you studied an epigenetic enzyme and found epigenetic genes changed—not surprising!)

Excellent approach:

  • Run RNA-seq → Get gene list → Validate top hits with RT-qPCR and Western blots
  • Identify specific candidate genes from enriched pathways based on biological knowledge
  • Test whether candidates are direct targets (ChIP-seq) or indirect effects
  • Functionally test whether preventing these changes rescues the phenotype
  • Build a mechanistic model with experimental support
  • Make testable predictions and validate them

The difference is biology, not bioinformatics. Both papers might have identical computational analysis. The excellent paper uses that analysis as a starting point for biological discovery.

Final Thoughts: Computation Enables Biology, But Doesn't Replace It

You Are a Biologist Who Uses Computational Tools

Not the other way around.

This tutorial has taught you how to go from raw FASTQ files to a statistically rigorous list of differentially expressed genes. That's an important skill, and you should feel confident using these computational approaches.

But never forget: The computational pipeline serves your biological understanding, not the other way around.

Your expertise as a biologist—knowing your cell type, your developmental stage, your signaling pathways, your phenotype, what's been published, what makes biological sense—is what transforms a gene list into biological insight.

A perfectly executed computational pipeline that produces biologically meaningless results is far worse than a rough-around-the-edges analysis that generates testable, important hypotheses.

Use these computational tools to ask better biological questions. Then get back in the lab to answer them.

The wet lab and the dry lab aren't separate—they're iterative partners in discovery.

Summary: Your RNA-seq Decision Framework

By now, you should have a clear mental framework for approaching RNA-seq analysis:

  1. Start with your biological question - this drives all downstream choices
  2. Consider your computational resources - be realistic about what you can run
  3. Choose alignment strategy - alignment-free for speed, alignment-based for flexibility
  4. Decide on resolution - gene-level for most questions, transcript-level when splicing matters
  5. Use appropriate normalization - raw counts for DE testing, normalized counts for visualization
  6. Account for experimental design - replicates, batches, paired samples
  7. Validate your results - QC at every step, visualize to ensure results make biological sense
  8. Remember: RNA ≠ protein ≠ function - computational analysis generates hypotheses that need experimental validation

Remember

There is no universally "correct" pipeline. The best approach depends on your specific question, your data characteristics, and your available resources. The goal is to make informed decisions that you can justify, rather than blindly following a recipe.

Most importantly: RNA-seq is a powerful discovery tool, but it's just the first step. The real scientific work—biological interpretation, experimental validation, and mechanistic understanding—comes from integrating computational analysis with wet lab experiments and deep knowledge of your biological system.
